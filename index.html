What is a PSU

A power supply converts alternating current (AC) to direct current (DC) , which is which they need computer components to function. Unlike some Hardware components whose use is not mandatory, the power supply is a crucial piece because without it, the rest of the internal hardware cannot function. Power supply is often abbreviated as PSU (from Power Supply Unit ).

This component mounts to the rear of the box or chassis. The side of the power supply that faces the outside of the case has a three-prong male port into which a power cable is plugged in and the other end is connected directly to the outlet current.

Inside the PC, a large number of cables run from the source. The connectors located on opposite ends of the cables connect to various components within the computer to supply themenergy .

Power supplies are rated by watts (W) to show how much power they can provide to computer. Since each part of the computer requires a certain amount of energy to function correctly, it is important to have a power supply that can provide the correct amount.

How does it works

The Power Supply Unit is a vital component for the operation of the computer. Power supplies use switching technology to convert current input alternating current, about 220 volts, at lower direct current voltages. The most used voltages are:

  • 3,3 volts
  • 5 volts
  • 12 volts

Nowadays about 90% or 95% of the load is on the 12V rail . Therefore, the other rails they remain in an increasingly secondary position. The wattage of a power supply is always shown in watts (W) . A watt is the product of the voltage in volts and the current in amps.

Currently, a computer is turned on with a small button and turned off with a menu option or with the own button. These options were built into the standard PSU several years ago. In this way, the operating system is able to send a signal to the PSU in order to indicate that it is turn off. The pushbutton sends a 5 volts signal to the power supply to tell it when to light up . The power supply also has a circuit that supplies stand-by power, called 5VSB (5 volts stand by) , even when the computer is turned off, so that devices that remain on standby can work and the source can be turned on.

The power supplies in use today are much lighter and smaller compared to the great weight and bulkiness of their ancestors. They transform the 60 Hz (cycles per second) current at a much higher frequency, resulting in more cycles per second. This conversion allows a small and lightweight transformer in the power supply to do the real voltage reduction of 220V (in Europe and most of the world) to the voltage necessary to saying particular component .

Keys for choosing a PSU

To choose a power supply correctly you must take into account three main concepts: power , amperage and efficiency .

  • Power and amperage : Power is measured in watts (W) and is the main criterion that follow users to When choosing a certain power supply for your computer. It is correct since it is a value to have note , but the problem is that in many cases it is not displayed realistically and I do not know complements appropriately with amperage , and the consequences of this can be dire .

  • Efficiency : it is a concept that in power supplies is defined as electrical energy lost in form of heat produced by the work of said component itself, that is, the power that loses What natural consequence of its own functioning . Efficency is rated by the 80 plus certification, that goes from 80 plus common to titanium rating. Types of PSU

  • MODULAR: Contains female connectors on the back to which only the cables required .
  • FLEX: This is a format that is used especially in pre-assembled equipment and also in Some servers , and it is not included among the most common formats since the availability of models at market is very limited . It should be mentioned that it is only used in models with very little capacity .
  • TFX: It stands out for its elongated size and its presentation like a shoe box, which wants say it's like a prism. They usually come with the fan arranged in the upper part , focused toward above.
  • SFX: It is a Small Form Factor (SFF, or Small Form Factor) , with power specifications almost identical to ATX and that, for Therefore, as a rule, they are fully compatible with their pins.
  • ATX: The most common standard today. It has been the direct evolution of the old AT format.
  • REDUNDANT: Set of two power supplies in one . Widely used in servers . If there is a bug in one of them, the other is still functional.
  • miniITX: For more compact and small computers, which require less power . Compatible with the miniITX box format.
  • microATX: Slightly smaller and with lower power than ATX.
